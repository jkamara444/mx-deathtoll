TARGETING

Who's Most at Risk and Why

Not everyone faces equal risk in Mexico's political violence. The targeting of journalists, activists, and political figures follows clear patterns based on what they investigate, who they challenge, and where they work. Understanding these patterns reveals the strategic nature of the violence and the specific threats that certain groups face.

The risk is not random, and instead needs to be understood as calculated and intentional. Those who investigate corruption, organize communities against powerful interests, or challenge political control are systematically targeted. The level of risk corresponds directly to the threat posed to established power structures, the flow of money, and the overall status quo.

Local Journalists

68%

Local reporters covering crime, politics, and corruption face the highest risk of violence. They work in their communities, making them easily identifiable and vulnerable to retaliation.

Risk Factors

  • Investigating organized crime
  • Reporting on political corruption
  • Covering cartel activities
  • Working in small communities

Environmental Defenders

42%

Activists opposing mining, logging, and other extractive industries face extreme violence from both criminal organizations and business interests with political connections.

Risk Factors

  • Opposing powerful corporations
  • Defending indigenous land rights
  • Challenging government contracts
  • Remote location vulnerability
  • Limited legal protection

Political Candidates

73%

Local political candidates, especially those challenging established parties or criminal influence, are routinely assassinated to control electoral outcomes.

Risk Factors

  • Running against incumbents
  • Opposing criminal-backed candidates
  • Local level campaigns
  • Limited security resources
  • Electoral cycle timing

Human Rights Defenders

28%

Those documenting abuses, defending marginalized communities, and seeking justice for victims face retaliation from both state and non-state actors.

Risk Factors

  • Documenting state violence
  • Defending migrant rights
  • Supporting victims' families
  • International advocacy work
  • Legal challenges to power

Women Journalists

35%

Female journalists face gender-specific threats including sexual violence, targeted harassment campaigns, and attacks on their families.

Risk Factors

  • Gender-based violence
  • Family targeting
  • Online harassment campaigns
  • Reporting on gender violence
  • Intersectional vulnerabilities

Indigenous Activists

31%

Indigenous leaders defending territory, language, and autonomy face violence from extractive industries and government development projects.

Risk Factors

  • Land rights defense
  • Resource extraction opposition
  • Cultural preservation work
  • Remote community locations
  • Limited state protection

The targeting patterns show a strategy of removing anyone who threatens power and the status quo. Local journalists who expose corruption, environmental defenders who challenge lucrative extraction projects, and political candidates who could disrupt criminal control of local governments are deliberately targeted.

This deliberate targeting serves several purposes. It removes immediate threats, spreads fear that keeps others silent, and strengthens the position of those who rely on violence to maintain control. The result is a society where self-censorship occurs and the truth is censored and prevented.
