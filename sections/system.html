THE SYSTEM

Structural Causes of Violence and Impunity

The violence against journalists, activists, and political figures does not occur in a vacuum. It operates within a complex system of institutional failure, corruption, and deliberate policy choices that enable and encourage attacks on those who threaten established power structures. Understanding this system is essential to understanding why the death toll continues to rise.

At its core, this system is designed to protect power through the strategic use of violence and the systematic denial of justice. It operates through multiple interconnected mechanisms that work together to create an environment where murderers face virtually no consequences while victims are permanently silenced through death or fear of death.

How the System Works

Power Threat Identified

Journalist investigating corruption, activist organizing against opposition, or politician challenging status quo

Fear Deployment

Powerful interests deploy threats, intimidation, and surveillance

Violence

Assassinations carried out with state complicity or state tolerance

Justice System Failure

Investigations stalled, evidence lost, witnesses intimidated

Impunity

Case is closed without justice, message of fear is sent to other activists and society

Impunity Infrastructure

95%

Mexico's justice system fails when it comes to political violence. Underfunded prosecutors, overloaded courts, and widespread corruption ensure impunity. This isn't a mistake as the system deliberately protects those who use violence to stay in power. Violence becomes an effective tool, not just against individual victims, but against communities, movements, and anyone who challenges authority.

Even federal investigations into journalist killings are often blocked or undermined by local officials with conflicts of interest or direct involvement in the crimes. Threats, harassment, and intimidation against journalists, activists, and society are normalized. Thus, creating a climate where fear silences opposition and justice is rarely had for victims.

State Complicity

30%

Investigations by international organizations show that's state is involved in at least 30% of documented cases. This includes direct action by police or military forces, as well as collusion between government officials and criminal groups.

The lines between state power and organized crime are deliberately blurred, allowing violence to be carried out with official protection while keeping plausible deniability.

Evidence points to systematic coordination between government agencies and criminal organizations to eliminate political opponents and control territory. This pattern goes beyond isolated cases, showing a form of state-sanctioned violence that operates with near-total impunity.

International investigations have documented federal police providing intelligence to criminal groups about journalist locations, helping facilitate their movements, and then failing to investigate attacks. This complicity turns political violence into a tool for maintaining political and economic power.

Institutional Weakness

$2.3M

The federal protection program in Mexico, officially called the Mechanism for Protection of Human Rights Defenders and Journalists, was created in 2012 to provide safety measures for journalists and activists that are at risk.

In 2023, the Mechanism received only $2.3 million which is far below what is needed to address the scale of crisis. Protection mechanisms are underfunded, understaffed, and often compromised by the very threats they are supposed to prevent.

As of November 2023, 651 journalists were enrolled in the Mechanism, including 469 men and 182 women. Requests for protection have risen sharply in recent years, and so have rejections: 14 in 2021, 49 in 2022, and another 49 in 2023.

Almost all journalists interviewed by Amnesty International and CPJ said that they continued to face security incidents even after enrolling in the Mechanism. Many described its response as slow, bureaucratic, and lacking in empathy. Female journalists in particular reported that staff minimized the risks they faced and failed to consider the impact of gender.

The federal protection mechanism has been criticized for reacting only after threats escalate, rather than preventing attacks in the first place.

Documented Evidence of Systemic Failure

Prosecuter Caseload

Mexico's criminal justice system rarely delivers justice. From 2010 through 2022, state prosecutors opened nearly 300,000 homicide investigations but formally identified a suspect and initiated criminal proceedings in only about 51,000 cases, a rate of roughly 17 %. This highlights how investigations stall, are underfunded, or fail before suspects are even named.

State and Police Involvement

Public officials and security forces are sometimes implicated in violent crimes, which complicates and derails investigations. When perpetrators include those responsible for enforcing the law, impartiality in investigations becomes unlikely.

Jurisdiction Conflicts

Fragmented authority between federal and local justice bodies creates confusion and delays. Overlapping responsibilities and poor coordination give powerful people room to slow or block investigations, especially when local interests are involved.

Witness Protection Failure

Witness protection in Mexico is weak and under-resourced, leaving people who cooperate with investigations vulnerable to intimidation or retaliation. Many witnesses refuse to come forward for fear of their safety which can undermine entire cases.

Justice System Impunity

The broader justice system consistently fails. Even in homicides, only about 17% of investigations result in suspects being formally identified and prosecuted. Cases that do reach court are often built on weak or compromised evidence, causing conviction to also be unlikely.

The Veracruz Pattern

Between 2010 and 2016, Veracruz became the deadliest state for journalists in the world. Governor Javier Duarte's administration created what investigators called a "perfect storm of impunity," a systematic campaign to silence critical reporting through violence while ensuring that investigations would fail.

Journalists investigating government corruption received threats and were then killed. Investigations were immediately compromised, evidence disappeared, and prosecutors deliberately stalled cases. At least 18 journalists were killed during this period, and there were zero convictions.

This was not random violence as Duarte's administration created an environment where killing journalists became an effective tool for maintaining power, backed by the state apparatus that was supposed to investigate these crimes.

The system that allows this violence to continue is self-reinforcing. Every killing that goes unpunished emboldens perpetrators and signals to potential victims that speaking out can lead to deadly risks. Every stalled or failed investigation reinforces the idea that violence is an effective tool for silencing dissent. Each journalist that is silenced strengthens the power of those who rely on fear to control information and maintain authority.

Addressing this crisis requires more than prosecuting individual cases. It demands deep, structural reform of the institutions that allow impunity to persist, including courts, prosecutor offices, and police. Until the underlying relationship between power and violence is challenged, threats to journalists will remain high and the death toll will continue to grow.
